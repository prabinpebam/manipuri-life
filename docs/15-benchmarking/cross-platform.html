Cross-Platform Lessons

Both reference games ship across PC, smartphone, and console - and their platform stories are instructive opposites. What Stardew Valley and Japanese Rural Life Adventure got right and wrong across platforms, distilled into decisions we must make now rather than patch later.

TL;DR

Multi-platform is a design-time decision, not a late port. Stardew grew PC-first and reached outward - great on PC, but mobile lags on updates, has no multiplayer, and touch fights its precision minigames; there is no cross-play anywhere. Japanese Rural Life Adventure went the other way: mobile-first on Apple Arcade, then PC and Switch - its streamlined touch UI travels well, yet the PC port drew scaling and control complaints for being a thin conversion. The lesson for us: fix the platform matrix and an input-agnostic, mobile-first UI before we build, or pay for it in rework.

Plan it, do not patch it

Every platform pain below traces back to a decision that could only have been made cheaply at the start: the input model, UI scaling, feature parity, and save portability. This page exists so those decisions land in our Cross-Platform Strategy from day one.

Two opposite platform stories

Stardew Valley - PC-first, ported outward

Built for keyboard and mouse, then brought to consoles (Switch, PlayStation, Xbox) and mobile (iOS, Android). PC is the lead platform: fastest updates, full mod support, best precision. The further a platform sits from that origin, the more it lags - mobile most of all.

Japanese Rural Life Adventure - mobile-first, ported up

Born on Apple Arcade for touch, then brought to PC (Steam) and Switch. Its menu-driven, low-precision design suits every input, so the core travels well - but the desktop port shipped with fullscreen and control rough edges that read as an unoptimized conversion.

Convergent lessons

Where a PC-first game and a mobile-first game agree, the lesson is load-bearing. Both point to the same truth: the input model and screen independence must be designed in, and no platform should be left to rot.

Do

  • Fix the platform matrix up front - which platforms, which is the lead, and each one's input model - before building UI or systems.
  • Design input-agnostic from day one. Every action must work with touch, gamepad, and keyboard/mouse: large tap targets, cursor-free navigation, no hover-only or right-click-only actions.
  • Build mobile-first, scale up. A UI built for the smallest screen and least precise input ports upward far better than a desktop UI ports down. JRLA's core proves it travels.
  • Resolution independence and true fullscreen with safe areas and dynamic UI scaling - designed in, not retrofitted.
  • Commit to feature and update parity. Ship features close together across platforms, or set expectations openly.
  • Portable, cloud-backed, versioned saves - and decide whether cross-save is a promise now, when it is cheap.
  • Keep core interactions phone-simple. One-handed, low-precision play also serves our cozy goal - the two aims reinforce each other.

Avoid

  • Desktop-first UI built on hover, right-click, and tiny targets that cannot reach touch or gamepad without a rebuild.
  • Precision-dependent minigames (Stardew's fishing and combat) that become miserable on a touchscreen.
  • Letting a platform fall far behind on updates - Stardew's mobile lag is a lasting source of community resentment.
  • One-platform-only features (mods) plus no cross-play, which splits the community.
  • Cursor-range clicking and fixed resolutions - the exact JRLA PC complaints.
  • Treating a port as an afterthought. A "barebones conversion" is visible to players and costs reviews.
  • Deferring the multiplayer and cross-play decision. Both are expensive to bolt on later; decide scope now.

The multiplayer and cross-play trap

Stardew shows that co-op and cross-play are structural: co-op exists on PC and console but never on mobile, and no version plays across platforms. Retrofitting either is costly. We must decide our multiplayer scope and any cross-play promise at design time, and let that decision shape the save system and netcode from the start.
