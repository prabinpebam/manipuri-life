Vision & Pillars

The north star and the handful of non-negotiable principles that every later decision - design, art, narrative, tech - has to answer to. This is an early brainstorm and first draft, put up for review.

TL;DR

A one-line vision (why this game exists) plus five design pillars that act as tie-breakers whenever two good options compete: Authenticity is the product, Belonging over accumulation, Calm is a feature, Understanding by living, and Specific, never generic. Everything here is a draft to react to - the point is to argue with it, cut it down, and sharpen it until each pillar earns its place.

Draft for review Nothing here is locked

This page is a starting position, not a decision. It exists so we have something concrete to disagree with. Expect names to change, pillars to merge or get cut, and the vision line to be rewritten. Once we converge, this becomes a P0 Foundation document that the whole project inherits - so it is worth getting the disagreements out now.

How to read this page

A vision-and-pillars document does two jobs, and it is easy to blur them. Keeping them separate is what makes the pillars useful rather than decorative.

The vision
One sentence that says why this game exists and what it should leave a player with. It is the emotional and cultural north star - inspiring, not operational.
The pillars
Three to five principles that are operational: each one is a test you can hold a real decision against and get a clear "more this / less that." If a pillar can't settle an argument, it isn't a pillar yet.

The test a good pillar has to pass

A pillar earns its place only if it can reject something attractive. "Fun" or "quality" are not pillars - nobody argues against them. "Belonging over accumulation" is a pillar because it will, at some point, make us cut a satisfying money-and-upgrades loop that any other cozy game would ship.

The vision - draft

A few phrasings of the same north star, from tightest to fullest. We pick and polish one; the rest are here to triangulate the feeling.

One line

Live an ordinary year in old Manipur - and come away knowing a real place, and its people, you never knew existed.

Elevator pitch

A calm, no-pressure life sim where you inhabit everyday life in the Meitei kingdom of Manipur - farming a little, cooking, joining festivals, and befriending your neighbours - and come to understand a real, almost-forgotten world simply by living inside it. It is a place to rest and a place to learn, and the two are the same act.

The change we want to make

Most people have never heard the word "Manipur." We want to be its warm, honest introduction to the world - and, for Manipuris, a way to touch their own history by living it rather than memorising it.

Ties back to the pitch

This vision is a condensation of What's the Proposal - the "playable ethnography" idea and the three-layer audience (anyone who needs to exhale, the Manipuri player, the curious world). If the vision and the pitch ever drift apart, one of them is wrong.

The five pillars - draft

Each pillar below has the same shape: what it means, why it matters, and - most importantly - what it rules out. The "rules out" line is the real content; it is how the pillar earns the right to exist.

1 · Authenticity is the product

What it means
Historical accuracy is a design constraint, not set dressing. Objects, food, tools, dress, customs, and conversation each answer a real question about how these people actually lived, governed by the Knowledge Bible and the decided 1850-1900 canonical era.
Why it matters
It is the one thing that makes this more than a reskinned farming game, and the reason historians and a community will trust it. Accuracy is the differentiator and the ethics.
What it rules out
Invented "exotic" flavour, borrowed festivals, anachronistic props for convenience. We allow a small, explicit playability budget (~2%) for deliberate, documented deviations - never silent ones.

2 · Belonging over accumulation

What it means
The core reward is being woven into a community - names remembered, help returned, a place at the festival - not stockpiles, upgrades, or an ever-growing farm. Progress is measured in relationships and understanding.
Why it matters
It is what makes the game Manipuri in feeling as well as fact: a life organised around kinship, reciprocity, and the lallup obligation, not around private optimisation.
What it rules out
A dopamine loop built on money and numbers going up; "efficiency" as the main verb; treating neighbours as vendors. If a system rewards hoarding over belonging, it fights this pillar.

3 · Calm is a feature

What it means
Restfulness is engineered on purpose: no fail states, no timers screaming, no punishment for taking your time. The game asks for your attention and gives you calm back.
Why it matters
Calm is the promise on the tin - the "break that actually restores you." It is also the honest register for a peaceful era of ordinary days.
What it rules out
Twitch challenge, scarcity pressure, missable content that induces anxiety, competitive scoring. Tension is allowed only as gentle, optional narrative weather - never as a stress mechanic.

4 · Understanding by living

What it means
The player learns a culture by inhabiting it - doing the day, keeping the calendar, sharing the meal - rather than by reading codex entries. Teaching is embedded in verbs, not in lectures.
Why it matters
"Learned by living it, not memorising it" is the whole educational thesis. It is also what makes the history stick emotionally.
What it rules out
Wall-of-text tutorials, quiz-like "did you learn?" checks, museum-label exposition dumps. Facts should arrive through action, place, and character first; reference text is a backstop, not the delivery.

5 · Specific, never generic

What it means
Everything reads as unmistakably Meitei Manipur - forms, palette, sound, language - and never as generic "Asian pastoral." Specificity is the aesthetic and the dignity.
Why it matters
A real, under-represented culture deserves to be shown on its own terms; specificity is also what makes the world feel true rather than assembled from stock parts.
What it rules out
Placeholder "generic village" art, pan-Asian mash-ups, decorative orientalism. When a shortcut would make the world less identifiably Manipuri, the shortcut loses.

Candidate pillars - to keep, cut, or fold in

Two more principles feel important but may belong inside the five above, or as process/tech commitments rather than experience pillars. Flagged here for the review to decide.

Made with, not just about

Manipuri memory, correction, and voice shape the game; it is co-authored with the community, not merely set there. Is this a pillar, or part of "Authenticity is the product" plus Cultural Integrity?

One valley, many platforms

The same cozy world holds together across PC, mobile, and console from day one. Feels more like a technical commitment than an experience pillar - keep it out of the core five?

How we'll use the pillars

Pillars are only worth writing if they change decisions. The intended workflow:

  1. Tie-breaker

    When two options are both "good," the one that serves more pillars wins. Pillars beat taste.

  2. Rejection test

    Any feature that violates a pillar needs an explicit, written exception (and usually spends the ~2% playability budget), or it is cut.

  3. Review lens

    Art, design, and narrative reviews ask "which pillar does this serve, and does anything here fight one?" before polish.

What contradicts what - tensions to resolve

Good pillars pull against each other; naming the tensions now stops them from being surprises later.

Authenticity vs. Calm

  • Real life in 1850-1900 had hard labour, illness, and loss. How much do we soften without lying?
  • Proposed default: depict honestly but keep it optional weather, never a stress mechanic.

Belonging vs. Player freedom

  • A community with real obligations (lallup, reciprocity) can feel like it constrains the player.
  • Proposed default: obligations are invitations with warmth, not fail-state chores.

Open questions for review

  • Five or fewer? Do any of the five collapse together (e.g. 4 and 5)? Fewer, sharper pillars are usually stronger.
  • Are the two candidates pillars or not? "Made with, not just about" and "One valley, many platforms" - promote, fold in, or move out?
  • Which vision phrasing? Pick one of the three drafts, or write a fourth.
  • Naming. Do the pillar names read as memorable and quotable, or too abstract?
  • Missing anything? Is there a principle the game clearly needs that none of these capture (sound? pacing? the role of the player-character)?
