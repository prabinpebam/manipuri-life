Vision & Pillars

The north star and the handful of non-negotiable principles that every later decision - design, art, narrative, tech - has to answer to. This is an early brainstorm and first draft, put up for review.

TL;DR

This has to be a great game first - one of the best in its category on pure craft, with or without the setting, for a global audience. The Meitei-Manipur world is a rich, distinctive bonus that most players will enjoy discovering - not the USP, and never an excuse for weak design. That stance leads a one-line vision plus six design pillars that act as tie-breakers: A great game first, Authenticity is the soul, not the sell, Progress you can see, Calm is a feature, Understanding by living, and Specific, never generic. Everything here is a draft to react to.

Draft for review Nothing here is locked

This page is a starting position, not a decision. It exists so we have something concrete to disagree with. Expect names to change, pillars to merge or get cut, and the vision line to be rewritten. Once we converge, this becomes a P0 Foundation document that the whole project inherits - so it is worth getting the disagreements out now.

How to read this page

A vision-and-pillars document does two jobs, and it is easy to blur them. Keeping them separate is what makes the pillars useful rather than decorative.

The vision
One sentence that says why this game exists and what it should leave a player with. It is the emotional and cultural north star - inspiring, not operational.
The pillars
A short set of principles that are operational: each one is a test you can hold a real decision against and get a clear "more this / less that." If a pillar can't settle an argument, it isn't a pillar yet.

The test a good pillar has to pass

A pillar earns its place only if it can reject something attractive. Bare "fun" or "quality" are not pillars - nobody argues against them. "A great game first" qualifies because it is a priority ordering, not a platitude: it rejects leaning on the Manipuri setting to excuse weak design. "Calm is a feature" qualifies because it will, at some point, make us cut a genuinely engaging timer or deadline that would juice retention in any other game.

The vision - draft

The stance: a great game first, for the world

Before anything else, this has to be a genuinely great game - one of the best in its category judged on feel, systems, pacing, and polish, with or without the Manipur context. It is built for a global audience with global ambition, not as a regional or educational niche. The Meitei-Manipur setting is an interesting bonus most players will be glad they discovered - it is the soul of the game's specificity, but it is not the USP. The test: if the history were the only reason to play, we would have failed - we should write a history book instead.

Don't confuse personal priority with the game's priority

A deliberate separation we hold on purpose. Personally, introducing Manipur to the world is the maker's top priority - it is the reason this project exists at all. But that cannot be the game's top priority. The game has to be good in its own right, as a game, first - only then has it earned the right to carry the Manipuri message to the world. People out there will not play it just because it is based on Manipur; a cultural mission strapped to a mediocre game reaches no one. Both truths live here at once: a deep personal purpose, and a ruthless product pragmatism that refuses to let the purpose excuse a weaker game. Keeping the two apart is exactly what makes the mission achievable.

The pillars - draft

Each pillar below has the same shape: what it means, why it matters, and - most importantly - what it rules out. The "rules out" line is the real content; it is how the pillar earns the right to exist. They are deliberately ordered: the game comes first.

1 · A great game first

What it means
It has to be one of the best games in its category on pure craft - feel, systems, pacing, and polish - and hold up as a great game with or without the Manipur setting, for a global audience. If it isn't a great game, nothing else on this page can save it.
Why it matters
The setting only reaches anyone if the game earns their time first. A worthy history, poorly played, teaches no one - if we can't nail the game, we should write a history book instead.
What it rules out
Leaning on cultural novelty to excuse weak design; shipping "important but not fun"; treating the Manipuri context as the USP or a reason to forgive rough gameplay. The setting is a bonus, never the crutch.

2 · Authenticity is the soul, not the sell

What it means
Historical accuracy is a design constraint, not set dressing - objects, food, tools, dress, customs, and conversation each answer a real question about how these people actually lived, governed by the Knowledge Bible and the decided 1850-1900 canonical era. It is the soul of the game's specificity, layered onto a game that already stands on its own.
Why it matters
Accuracy is what earns the trust of historians and the community and gives the world a texture no invented setting can fake - the bonus that turns a great cozy game into a memorable one. It deepens the experience; it does not have to carry it.
What it rules out
Treating accuracy as the headline feature or the excuse for thin gameplay - and, equally, inventing "exotic" flavour, borrowing festivals, or sanding off real detail for convenience. We allow a small, explicit playability budget (~2%) for deliberate, documented deviations - never silent ones.

3 · Progress you can see - goals worth chasing

What it means
Give players concrete, trackable goals at every scale - granular (this recipe, this fish, one friendship deepened) and macro (every fish in Loktak, all the costumes, every hidden shrine, the full festival calendar). Progress is visible and legible: counters, collections, journals, milestones. And belonging is itself a first-class, measurable goal - relationship tracks, favours owed and repaid, roles earned at festivals - not an unmeasured "warm feeling." Accumulation is welcome; what matters is what you accumulate and why.
Why it matters
Many players - especially in this genre - are driven by goals and completion; watching the bar fill is the engine that keeps them in the world, and we will not throw those players away. Better still, every Manipuri collectible is a Trojan horse for real knowledge: to "collect them all" you learn Loktak's fish, the weave of a phanek, the shrines of the valley. Goals and the cultural mission pull in the same direction.
What it rules out
Not accumulation - empty accumulation. Numbers that climb with no meaning, wealth-maxing that turns neighbours into vendors, efficiency that crowds out the human and cultural core. Every goal should either connect you to people or teach you something about this world; a goal that does neither is filler. And belonging must be as trackable as any collection - vibes are not a system.

4 · Calm is a feature

What it means
Restfulness is engineered on purpose: no fail states, no timers screaming, no punishment for taking your time. The game asks for your attention and gives you calm back.
Why it matters
Calm is the promise on the tin - the "break that actually restores you." It is also the honest register for a peaceful era of ordinary days.
What it rules out
Twitch challenge, scarcity pressure, missable content that induces anxiety, competitive scoring. Tension is allowed only as gentle, optional narrative weather - never as a stress mechanic.

5 · Understanding by living

What it means
The player learns a culture by inhabiting it - doing the day, keeping the calendar, sharing the meal - rather than by reading codex entries. Teaching is embedded in verbs, not in lectures.
Why it matters
"Learned by living it, not memorising it" is the whole educational thesis. It is also what makes the history stick emotionally.
What it rules out
Wall-of-text tutorials, quiz-like "did you learn?" checks, museum-label exposition dumps. Facts should arrive through action, place, and character first; reference text is a backstop, not the delivery.

6 · Specific, never generic

What it means
Everything reads as unmistakably Meitei Manipur - forms, palette, sound, language - and never as generic "Asian pastoral." Specificity is the aesthetic and the dignity.
Why it matters
A real, under-represented culture deserves to be shown on its own terms; specificity is also what makes the world feel true rather than assembled from stock parts.
What it rules out
Placeholder "generic village" art, pan-Asian mash-ups, decorative orientalism. When a shortcut would make the world less identifiably Manipuri, the shortcut loses.

Reframed pillar 3 to "Progress you can see" (was "Belonging over accumulation")

An earlier draft pitted belonging against accumulation. That was too absolutist: goal-driven and completionist players are core to this genre, and "collect them all" is one of our best teaching tools, not an enemy of the mission - chasing every fish, costume, or secret shrine is chasing more of Manipur. The real line isn't belonging vs. accumulation; it's meaningful goals - belonging included and made measurable - vs. soulless optimisation.

Added "A great game first" as the lead pillar; reframed authenticity

Introduced "A great game first" as pillar 1 and renamed "Authenticity is the product" to "Authenticity is the soul, not the sell" - accuracy deepens a game that already stands on its own, rather than being the USP.

How we'll use the pillars

Pillars are only worth writing if they change decisions. The intended workflow:

  1. Tie-breaker

    When two options are both "good," the one that serves more pillars wins. Pillars beat taste.

  2. Rejection test

    Any feature that violates a pillar needs an explicit, written exception (and usually spends the ~2% playability budget), or it is cut.

  3. Review lens

    Art, design, and narrative reviews ask "which pillar does this serve, and does anything here fight one?" before polish.

What contradicts what - tensions to resolve

Good pillars pull against each other; naming the tensions now stops them from being surprises later.

Authenticity vs. Calm

  • Real life in 1850-1900 had hard labour, illness, and loss. How much do we soften without lying?
  • Proposed default: depict honestly but keep it optional weather, never a stress mechanic.

Belonging vs. Player freedom

  • A community with real obligations (lallup, reciprocity) can feel like it constrains the player.
  • Proposed default: obligations are invitations with warmth, not fail-state chores.

Open questions for review

  • Is "A great game first" a pillar or the frame? It may be the meta-principle the other five serve, rather than one pillar among equals. Keep it numbered, or lift it above the list?
  • Six or fewer? Do any collapse together (e.g. Understanding by living and Specific, never generic)? Fewer, sharper pillars are usually stronger.
  • Are the two candidates pillars or not? "Made with, not just about" and "One valley, many platforms" - promote, fold in, or move out?
  • Which vision phrasing? Pick one of the two one-liners (or write a third), and confirm the pitch leads clearly with the game, not the setting.
  • Naming. Do the pillar names read as memorable and quotable, or too abstract?
  • Missing anything? Is there a principle the game clearly needs that none of these capture (sound? pacing? the role of the player-character)?
