Process

How We Work

A nine-phase method for turning history into a game without inventing it. This is the operating system behind every document on this site.

TL;DR

Pick one specific year, set a strict 98/2 reality-to-convenience ratio, build a Knowledge Bible, research like an anthropologist, capture every fact in a structured database with a confidence rating, and derive game mechanics from history rather than reskinning Stardew.

The nine phases

  1. Phase 1 — Define the historical snapshot

    First choose the era — see Choosing the Era, where we weigh five fifty-year windows from 1700 to 1950. Then narrow it to one specific year (e.g. 1728, 1815, or 1890). Each year fixes the religion, the wars, the festivals, the script, the crops, and the roads — which collapses ambiguity everywhere else.

  2. Phase 2 — Decide the fantasy level

    98% historical reality, 2% gameplay convenience. No magic, no monsters. Folklore, dreams, festivals and beliefs exist exactly as people believed them — a player may hear a ghost story without ever proving ghosts are real.

  3. Phase 3 — Build the Knowledge Bible

    One structured source of truth spanning World, Society, Economy, Government, Architecture, Daily Life, Nature, Language, and the Historical Timeline. Everything eventually plugs into gameplay.

  4. Phase 4 — Research like an anthropologist

    Kings are ~1% of the game. Study how people actually swept floors, preserved fish, carried water, stored rice, proposed marriage, mourned the dead, and repaired roofs. The tiny details create immersion.

  5. Phase 5 — Gather five kinds of sources

    Primary historical accounts, indigenous sources (incl. the royal chronicle Cheitharol Kumbaba), anthropology, archaeology, and living traditions. Cross-reference before trusting.

  6. Phase 6 — Build a Historical Database

    Every fact becomes a structured object — description, year range, confidence, sources, evidence, and downstream gameplay / art / dialogue ideas. Over time this becomes the "world engine."

  7. Phase 7 — Design mechanics from history

    Ask "what activities actually filled people's days?" The answer is the systems list: rice cultivation, weaving, fishing, Lallup service, festivals, markets, marriage negotiations.

  8. Phase 8 — Design the emotional arc

    The reward is belonging and legacy, not accumulation: a respected household, children who inherit it, a community irrigation system, or a name kept in village oral tradition.

  9. Phase 9 — Build an advisory circle

    Bring historians, archaeologists, linguists, craftspeople, and elders in from the start, not as end-stage fact-checkers. Let them challenge assumptions early.

The 98 / 2 rule, concretely

Allowed (historical reality)

  • Folklore, dreams, festivals, and beliefs as believed
  • Real crops, tools, foods (ngari), clothing (phanek, pheijom)
  • Real institutions — Lallup service, women-run markets, the king's court
  • Period-accurate architecture, language, and script

Forbidden / convenience-only

  • No magic, no fantasy creatures, no mythical monsters as real
  • Minor time compression for a fun daily loop (the 2%)
  • Gentle UI affordances that never contradict the record
  • No anachronistic crops, tools, or customs "because they're fun"

Confidence model

A fact's confidence governs how prominently it may drive gameplay. Low-confidence facts can flavour a background, but must never be a load-bearing mechanic.

High Multiple independent sources agree — safe as a core mechanic.

Medium One solid source or strong inference — usable, flagged for review.

Low / Speculative Single weak source or extrapolation — background flavour only, never a core system.

The test that matters

If players finish the game feeling they have lived in historical Manipur — not merely visited a picturesque version of it — the method worked.
