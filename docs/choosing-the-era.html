Choosing the Era

The game is set in the historical Meitei kingdom of Manipur — but when is still undecided. We are weighing five fifty-year windows between 1700 and 1950. This page is the framework for that choice, with honest notes on each era to argue for and against.

TL;DR

Picking the era is the first foundational decision — it fixes the religion, the wars, the script, the clothing, the monuments, and above all the mood. We are choosing between five windows: 1700–1750, 1750–1800, 1800–1850, 1850–1900, and 1900–1950. For a calm, cozy life-sim the two strongest candidates are 1850–1886 (Chandrakirti Singh) — the most stable and materially rich window — and 1770–1795 (Bhagyachandra), the artistic golden age that gave Manipur its classical Raas Leela dance. Nothing is locked yet; the notes below are how we decide.

Why this page exists

Everything downstream — research depth, art direction, costumes, architecture, festivals, even the script on signs — depends on the year. Rather than quietly assume "the 1700s," we are making the era an explicit, reversible decision with the trade-offs written down. See also How We Work, Phase 1.

Why the era matters

Manipur changed profoundly across these 250 years: an indigenous Sanamahi kingdom with its own Meitei Mayek script became a Vaishnav-Hindu state; it was nearly destroyed by a seven-year Burmese occupation, rebuilt, and then absorbed into British India. A single decade can be the difference between a peaceful market town and a war-emptied valley. The era we pick decides:

Religion & ritual
Sanamahi household worship and Lai Haraoba, Vaishnav Krishna festivals and Raas Leela, or the lived tension between the two.
Script & language on every sign
Meitei Mayek before the 1729 book-burning; Bengali script after. This shows up on every letter, ledger, and shopfront in the game.
Who holds power
An independent Meitei king, a Burmese occupier, or a British Political Agent behind a figurehead maharaja.
What the world looks like
Which temples, forts, and markets stand — the Govindajee Temple (1846) and the Kangla Sha statues (1804) simply do not exist in the earliest eras.
The emotional register
Whether the backdrop is calm and cozy, or shadowed by invasion, famine, and colonial loss.

The decision framework

We score each era against the qualities a cozy, historically-honest life-sim actually needs. No single axis wins on its own; the goal is a window that is peaceful enough to relax in, rich enough to be worth depicting, and respectful of living memory.

1 · Coziness & peace
Is daily life in this window calm, or overshadowed by war, occupation, or famine? A cozy game needs long stretches of ordinary time.
2 · Cultural richness
How much festival, craft, music, dance, and ritual life is there to draw on? More texture means more things for a player to discover.
3 · Visual distinctiveness
Does the era look unmistakably Manipuri — costume, architecture, landscape — rather than generic?
4 · Source availability
Can we actually research it well? Later eras (esp. post-1850) have far richer records, ethnography, and photographs.
5 · Sensitivity load
How much of the era is a living cultural wound (the 1729 book-burning, the 1819–26 devastation, the 1891 annexation, WWII, the 1949 merger)? Higher load means more care — or a reason to steer around it.

Note on sensitivity

Several events below are living cultural wounds in Meitei memory — the 1729 Puya Mei Thaba (burning of manuscripts), the 1819–1826 devastation, the 1891 annexation, and the WWII Battle of Imphal. They are named here so the decision is informed, not to be used as casual backdrop. Whichever era we choose, our Cultural Integrity guidelines govern how it is handled.

Era 1 · 1700–1750 — the great conversion

The era of Meidingu Pamheiba (Garib Niwaz, r. 1709–1751), the most transformative — and most disruptive — king in Meitei history. It opens on a fully functioning indigenous Sanamahi kingdom and closes on a state that has been forcibly turned toward Vaishnav Hinduism. It is simultaneously the height of Meitei royal power and a cultural rupture.

Who was in power

Charairongba (c. 1697–1709), then his son Pamheiba / Garib Niwaz (1709–1751), initiated into Vaishnavism by the missionary Shantidas Gosai around 1717.

Major events

  • ~1717 — royal conversion to Gaudiya Vaishnavism (Krishna worship).
  • ~1729 — the Puya Mei Thaba: a decree to burn Meitei-script religious manuscripts; Bengali script progressively imposed. (Scale is debated by scholars.)
  • 1720s–1740s — repeated wars against Burma and the Ahom kingdom of Assam.
  • ~1751 — Pamheiba dies; a decade of succession instability follows.

Daily life & economy

  • The lallup corvée system dominates — every able man owed the state roughly 10 days' labour in every 40; because men were often away, women ran the households and the markets (the roots of Ima Keithel).
  • Paddy and black chak-hao rice, cotton weaving of phanek and innaphi, bamboo basketry, pottery, iron tools; ngari (fermented fish) as staple protein and trade good; Loktak Lake fishing on floating phumdi.

Religion & culture

  • Before 1717: full Sanamahi life — household guardian worship, Umang Lai sacred groves, the Lai Haraoba festival, Thang-Ta martial arts.
  • After: Vaishnavism enforced from the court down; indigenous practice pushed into the household and the village edges. Raas Leela does not exist yet.
  • Sagol Kangjei (Meitei polo) already ancient and unaffected.

Monuments & architecture

  • Kangla Fort — the ancient walled royal capital (major brickwork by Khagemba, r. 1597–1654), with moat, gates, and shrines.
  • Sanamahi household shrines and sacred groves are the dominant "religious architecture." No large Vaishnav temples yet; the Kangla Sha statues (1804) and Govindajee Temple (1846) are still a century off.
  • Villages: bamboo-frame, thatched, east-facing houses with woven walls and a central hearth.

Cozy fit

A split era. The pre-conversion sub-window 1700–1717 is genuinely cozy and visually unique (Meitei Mayek everywhere, Lai Haraoba, no Krishna iconography). After 1729 the mood turns anxious. The 1729 book-burning is a deep wound — set here only with real care, or in a village far from court.

Era 2 · 1750–1800 — the artistic golden age

After a decade of instability, the era centres on the long reign of the saint-king Bhagyachandra (Ching-Thang Khomba / Jai Singh, main reign 1763–1798). Despite Burmese pressure and an exile, his court produces the golden age of Manipuri Vaishnav arts — above all the birth of classical Raas Leela dance.

Who was in power

A fragile 1751–1759 window of short-reigned claimants, then Bhagyachandra (1759–1761, then 1763–1798 after being restored with Ahom help).

Major events

  • ~1762 — a major Burmese invasion drives Bhagyachandra into exile in Assam.
  • ~1763 — restored to the throne with Ahom military support; rules for 35 years.
  • ~1779 onward — by tradition, a vision of Krishna in exile leads him to found the Raas Leela and install Govindajee (Krishna) worship on his return.
  • 1798 — Bhagyachandra dies; a fratricidal succession begins that leads into the next era's catastrophe.

Daily life & economy

  • Lallup and the women's market economy continue in full; handloom weaving is a primary women's craft.
  • Manipuri ponies are a valued export and the basis of the polo tradition; trade runs to Burma (Chindwin route) and Cachar/Assam.

Religion & culture

  • Vaishnavism now entrenched as the state religion, with Sanamahi surviving at household level.
  • Raas Leela founded (~1779–80s): Krishna's circular dance with Radha and the Gopis, female dancers in the cylindrical kumil skirt — the defining form of Manipuri cultural identity.
  • Polo, Thang-Ta, kirtan singing, and the royal chronicle (Cheitharol Kumbaba) all active.

Monuments & architecture

  • Kangla still the royal seat; Bhagyachandra installs Govindajee worship there (the permanent temple comes later, in 1846).
  • Open bamboo mandap pavilions for Raas Leela begin appearing in villages; houses remain bamboo-and-thatch.

Cozy fit — strong candidate

1770–1795 under Bhagyachandra may be the most aesthetically vibrant window of the 18th century: Raas Leela evenings, riverside markets, polo on the plain, classical music at court. Burmese war sits at the edges as distant news rather than daily threat. Bhagyachandra is revered as a saint-king — depict him with respect.

Era 3 · 1800–1850 — devastation and recovery

The darkest era in modern Meitei history. Fratricidal civil war and repeated Burmese invasions culminate in the Chahi Taret Khuntakpa — the "Seven Years' Devastation" (1819–1826) — when Burma occupies Manipur and the valley is nearly depopulated. The second half is a hard-won recovery under Gambhir Singh and Nara Singh.

Who was in power

Warring sons of Bhagyachandra (Chourjit, Marjit); Burmese-installed governors (1819–1825); Gambhir Singh (main reign 1825–1834, the liberation hero); then Nara Singh (regent 1834–1844, king 1844–1850).

Major events

  • 1819–1826 — the Seven Years' Devastation: mass killing, enslavement, and flight; the Imphal valley largely emptied.
  • 1824 — Gambhir Singh allies with the British in the First Anglo-Burmese War, raising the Manipur Levy.
  • 24 Feb 1826 — the Treaty of Yandabo: Burma cedes its claim; Manipur becomes a British protectorate.
  • 16 Jan 1846 — Nara Singh commissions the Shree Govindajee Temple, Imphal — the central royal temple of Manipuri Vaishnavism.

Daily life & economy

  • 1800–1826: near-total collapse — agriculture fails as men fight or flee; famine follows; refugees scatter to Cachar, Tripura, and Assam.
  • 1826–1850: repopulation and restart — Gambhir and Nara Singh coax the displaced home and rebuild the rice economy; lallup continues.

Monuments & architecture

  • Kangla restored by Gambhir Singh after occupation.
  • The Govindajee Temple (1846) is the landmark of the recovery; a British Residency appears at Imphal.
  • Villages rebuilt in bamboo-and-thatch, materials sparse and improvised.

Cozy fit — weakest of the five

1800–1826 is emphatically not cozy. The only viable window is the 1840s recovery, whose "return and rebuild a broken valley" story is poignant but not relaxing. The devastation is a sacred wound in Meitei memory and must never be an adventure backdrop.

Era 4 · 1850–1900 — the stable golden valley

The most stable and materially rich window for a village life-sim. The long, peaceful reign of Chandrakirti Singh (1850–1886) brings agricultural plenty, patronage of the arts, and settled Vaishnav cultural life — before the turbulence of the 1891 Anglo-Manipur War closes the century.

Who was in power

Chandrakirti Singh (1850–1886); then a short, unstable succession (Surachandra, Kulachandra, the Senapati Tikendrajit) ending in the Anglo-Manipur War of 1891 and the British-installed child-king Churachandra.

Major events

  • 1868 — a major earthquake damages the Govindajee Temple.
  • 1876 — the temple is rebuilt and re-consecrated (the structure that, modified, stands today).
  • 1890–91 — palace coup, then the Anglo-Manipur War; British victory, the public hanging of Tikendrajit and Thangal General (13 Aug 1891, now Patriots' Day), and the occupation of Kangla.
  • 1892 — the British abolish the lallup system.

Daily life & economy

  • Peak recovery: thriving paddy cultivation, multiple rice varieties, ceremonial chak-hao, cotton and silk weaving, Loktak fishing.
  • Ima Keithel and the women's markets flourish; the Manipuri pony is traded and raced; printed currency slowly enters alongside barter.

Religion & culture

  • Vaishnavism deeply embedded — Yaoshang (Meitei Holi), Janmashtami, Jhulan Yatra; Raas Leela at the Govindajee Temple and village mandaps.
  • Sanamahi and Vaishnav life settle into a stable coexistence; Lai Haraoba continues more openly.
  • Polo at its peak — British officers watch the Meitei game in this era and carry modern polo to the world.

Monuments & architecture

  • Kangla as royal capital (until the 1891 British occupation); the golden-domed Govindajee Temple (1876) as the heart of Imphal.
  • One of the world's oldest continuously used polo grounds; village mandaps everywhere; a modest British Residency.

Cozy fit — the front-runner

1855–1885 under Chandrakirti is the single strongest window across all five eras: the most stable, richest, and best-documented. Imphal valley in full bloom — rice paddies, doorstep weaving, morning fish markets, Raas Leela evenings, polo on the maidan, golden temple domes, Loktak's floating phumdi. A natural seasonal calendar for gameplay, with the coming British encroachment as quiet narrative tension.

Era 5 · 1900–1950 — village life under the colonial shadow

Manipur is now a British-supervised princely state: a maharaja in the palace, a Political Agent whose word carries further. Village life keeps its traditional rhythm under a slowly modernising surface — and Meitei women twice rise in the Nupi Lan (Women's Wars) before the era ends in the cataclysm of WWII and the 1949 merger with India.

Who was in power

Churachandra Singh (1891–1941) and Bodhachandra Singh (1941–1955), under the real authority of the British Political Agent and the Manipur State Darbar.

Major events

  • 1904 — the First Nupi Lan: thousands of market women force the colonial administration to withdraw a forced-labour order.
  • 1917–18 — the Manipur Labour Corps (~2,000 men) sent to WWI Europe and Mesopotamia.
  • Dec 1939 — the Second Nupi Lan: a mass women's protest over rice export and scarcity becomes a demand for reform.
  • Mar–Jul 1944 — the Battle of Imphal: Japanese and INA forces besiege the city; civilians flee; Japan's worst defeat.
  • 15 Oct 1949 — Manipur merges into the Indian Union; the monarchy ends.

Daily life & economy

  • Farming still the heart of life; after lallup's 1892 abolition, men's labour returns to the fields — but colonial taxes and a cash economy bring new pressures.
  • Ima Keithel is both marketplace and political engine — the Nupi Lan grew out of the market women's networks.
  • Modest modern texture: roads, post, telegraph, kerosene lamps, the occasional vehicle in town; handloom weaving becomes a marker of cultural identity.

Religion & culture

  • Vaishnav festivals continue; an early Sanamahi revival and cultural nationalism begin as intellectuals question the imposed religion and advocate reviving Meitei Mayek.
  • Manipuri classical dance reaches wider India via Rabindranath Tagore (~1919, Shantiniketan).
  • Thang-Ta, suppressed after 1891, is practised quietly and slowly revives.

Monuments & architecture

  • Kangla is off-limits — held by the Assam Rifles and forbidden to Manipuris (not returned until 2004), a constant undercurrent of cultural grief.
  • The Govindajee Temple remains the cultural centre; colonial buildings and, after 1944, the Imphal War Cemetery enter the landscape; villages keep bamboo-and-thatch with some tin roofing.

Cozy fit — workable with care

1905–1935 — after the first Nupi Lan, before WWII — is a manageable cozy window: village rhythms intact, vibrant market culture, a little modern texture. The Nupi Lan make powerful civic set pieces. Steer clear of 1939–1945. The 1949 merger and WWII trauma both need a careful hand.

Where we're leaning

Current front-runners (not a final decision)

1. Era 4 — Chandrakirti's Manipur (1850–1886). The most stable, materially rich, and best-documented window; the closest thing to a ready-made cozy calendar, with British encroachment as gentle late-game tension.
2. Era 2 — Bhagyachandra's Manipur (1770–1795). More artistically vibrant — the birth of Raas Leela — at the cost of Burmese-war anxiety at the margins and thinner sources.

The earliest era (1700–1717) is a tempting outlier for its wholly indigenous, pre-Vaishnav look, but its short cozy window and the weight of the 1729 rupture make it a harder foundation. Eras 3 and the WWII end of Era 5 are the least suited to a relaxing game.

Open questions before we lock it

  • How much colonial presence do we want? None (Eras 1–2), a light protectorate touch (late Era 4), or an explicit colonial backdrop (Era 5)?
  • Indigenous vs. Vaishnav aesthetic: the Meitei-Mayek, Sanamahi look of Era 1, or the Krishna-festival, Raas-Leela look of Eras 2–5?
  • Do we want a real monument the player recognises? The Govindajee Temple only exists from 1846 onward.
  • Which sub-window, not just which fifty years — the cozy pocket inside each era is narrower than the era itself.
  • Advisory review: any chosen era should be sanity-checked with historians and community elders before it is locked (see How We Work, Phase 9).
